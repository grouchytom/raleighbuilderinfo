Homeowner's Guide to choosing a builder in Raleigh

Building a home is likely the largest financial commitment you'll make. This guide walks through what to check before signing a contract — and how to use this dashboard at each step. North Carolina has specific licensing requirements that give you meaningful tools to protect yourself.

1
Verify the license — before anything else
In North Carolina, any contractor working on a project over $40,000 is legally required to hold a license from the NC Licensing Board for General Contractors. An unlicensed builder exposes you to serious financial and legal risk — if something goes wrong, you have no recourse and you may be liable for injuries on your own property.
Look for the Accredited Builder or Accredited Master Builder designation from the NC Builder Institute — these signal ongoing education and commitment to professional standards. Licensed contractors are also required to carry general liability insurance and complete at least 8 hours of continuing education per year to renew.
In this dashboard: Search the builder → open their profile → click NC License Board to verify their license status and check for complaints. The license number is shown in the profile header if the permit data includes it.
2
Look at their track record in the permit data
The permit record is an unfiltered history of every home a builder has started in Raleigh since 2020. Unlike reviews, it can't be curated. Key things to look for:
  • Completion rate — what share of their permitted homes reach "Permit Finaled" status? A builder with many stalled or expired permits is a concern.
  • Avg. build time — Raleigh new construction typically runs 8–14 months. Builders consistently over 19 months warrant a conversation about why.
  • Volume of work — a builder with 50+ active permits may be stretched thin. A builder with only 1–2 may lack experience. Neither is disqualifying, but both are worth asking about.
  • Quality flags — stalled permits (issued over a year ago with no completion) may indicate financial trouble, permitting issues, or over-commitment.
In this dashboard: Use the Build time and Done columns in the table. Sort by Quality to surface builders with flags. Click any builder to see their full project history with individual permit links.
3
Check inspection history for each permit
Inspection records reveal whether a builder's work consistently meets code on the first attempt. Repeated failed inspections — especially on the same type of work (framing, electrical, plumbing) — suggest systemic quality problems, not just bad luck. Local building inspectors see every builder's work regularly and can tell you which contractors routinely fail before meeting code.
What to look for: a high ratio of re-inspections to total inspections is a red flag. One or two failed inspections on a large custom build is normal. Five or more on a standard production home is not.
In this dashboard: Click any permit number in a builder's project list. The Raleigh permit portal opens to that permit — click the Inspections tab to see the full pass/fail history. No login required.
4
Search for legal issues, liens, and court cases
Mechanics liens are filed when subcontractors or suppliers aren't paid — if a builder has multiple liens, it may signal cash flow problems that could affect your project mid-build. Civil court cases may reveal disputes with previous clients. Neither is automatically disqualifying, but patterns matter.
Also check the NC Secretary of State to confirm the business is in good standing — a dissolved or administratively revoked entity is a significant warning sign.
In this dashboard: In the builder profile → Verify this builder → use the NC Courts & Liens, NC Secretary of State, and NC License Board links. Search by the exact company name shown in the profile.
5
Talk to owners of recently completed homes
No data source replaces a direct conversation with someone who has lived in a home the builder built. Ask specifically: Did they hit the timeline? How did they handle problems? Would you hire them again? A good builder will proactively offer references. Be cautious of any builder who hesitates.
The Raleigh area has an active homeowner community — the Raleigh-Wake County Home Builders Association is the second largest HBA in the country and a good resource for finding vetted builders and understanding industry norms.
In this dashboard: Find a completed project in a builder's profile → click Current owner (Wake Co.) → to open the Wake County property record. The current owner's name and mailing address are public record — you can reach out directly to ask about their experience.
6
Red flags to walk away from
  • Asks you to pull the permits yourself — this is a sign the builder may not be licensed. Licensed builders pull their own permits.
  • Requests large upfront payments — a reasonable payment schedule ties payments to construction milestones, not calendar dates.
  • No written contract or vague scope — every detail should be in writing: materials, timeline, payment schedule, change order process, and warranty terms.
  • Pressures you to sign quickly — reputable builders stay busy and don't need to rush you. Pressure to decide is a sales tactic, not a sign of quality.
  • Can't provide references — any builder with a real track record will have happy clients willing to talk.
  • No insurance documentation — ask for a certificate of insurance before signing. Verify it directly with the insurer.
  • Unusually low bid — if one estimate is significantly below the others, find out why. It often means cut corners on materials, unlicensed subcontractors, or a builder who will come back for change orders.
